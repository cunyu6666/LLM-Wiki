# The way we design AI looks like nostalgia already

[www.freedium.cfd](https://www.freedium.cfd/https://medium.com/user-experience-design-1/the-way-we-design-ai-looks-like-nostalgia-already-aa5dfc01d4c7)

AI is new and exciting, but its visual branding has become redundant. Despite how powerful and versatile AI is, the way most companies brand it is dull, repetitive, and doomed to look outdated soon.

Common elements in AI marketing designs include:

* Starbursts
* Glow
* Purple gradients

It almost seems like there is an unspoken requirement for companies to refresh their brand guidelines to incorporate these elements. Today, we will take a look at this trend and see if the design patterns are a sustainable solution for companies involved in AI.

### Examples

#### Adobe

Adobe has decided to include its AI component in Acrobat with a prominent button that is now visually the focus of the app. The design contains the trendy ingredients mentioned and takes user focus away from the rest of the UI. What does the button do? No way to tell as there are no clear cues, besides the chat bubble icon that might imply that it is a chatbot or such.

#### Microsoft

As one of the earliest players in the AI space, Microsoft decided to give its AI offering a unique identity. The designs contain all the expected visuals from this trend as well. Microsoft's AI has evolved from Bing Chat to Copilot but elements like gradients and starbursts remain.

![](https://cubox.pro/c/filters:no_upscale()?imageUrl=https%3A%2F%2Fmiro.medium.com%2Fv2%2Fresize%3Afit%3A700%2F1%2Abro81DTaVz5_AA70sF2fng.png&valid=true)

#### Google

Google also gave its AI offering a unique name and went through a rebrand exercise from Bard to Gemini. The design style remains mostly the same, once again, with starbursts and purple gradients.

![](https://cubox.pro/c/filters:no_upscale()?imageUrl=https%3A%2F%2Fmiro.medium.com%2Fv2%2Fresize%3Afit%3A700%2F1%2AtoCClyiabUwS_v4tx8ivaQ.png&valid=true)

#### Meta, Apple, Jasper, and More

There are tons of other examples born from this design trend, such as AI from Meta, Jasper, Zoom, Opera, GitHub, and more. Tell me these marketing assets don't look like they are all from the same company.

![](https://cubox.pro/c/filters:no_upscale()?imageUrl=https%3A%2F%2Fmiro.medium.com%2Fv2%2Fresize%3Afit%3A700%2F1%2A2KmT9Aa2gD-bCCjIROkT9Q.png&valid=true)

Apple even gave up on its iconic Siri design for an icon that almost seems like it's designed to be more aligned with the AI design trend.

![](https://cubox.pro/c/filters:no_upscale()?imageUrl=https%3A%2F%2Fmiro.medium.com%2Fv2%2Fresize%3Afit%3A700%2F1%2AG9RKTAt8k33ZoBIeWu-waA.png&valid=true)

To be clear, the purpose of this article is not to make fun of the look of this design trend. The trend is visually pleasing, and there may a good reason to adopt it. As [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2024/06/15/apple-joins-the-race-to-find-an-ai-icon-that-makes-sense/ "") pointed out, we are in an era where companies are figuring out what an AI icon should look like. It makes sense for companies to adopt what seems to be the most popular way to represent AI so users can quickly identify the AI offerings...Or does it?

### What's the issue?

It is now a trend that is doomed to look outdated soon.

Whether using these common AI elements in marketing makes sense or not, given the popularity, they will soon look like nostalgia. When every designer is making decisions influenced by social norms or culture instead of usability, a design trend is born. And a trend does what a trend does, it becomes popular and quickly fades away.

This is Frutiger Aero\* all over again.

Frutiger Aero. Via [Fandom](https://aesthetics.fandom.com/wiki/Frutiger_Aero "")

*\** [Frutiger Aero](https://aesthetics.fandom.com/wiki/Frutiger_Aero "")*is a design trend that became popular in the mid-2000s. It is characterized by glossy texture, tropical vibes, bubbles, lens flare, and such.*

While Frutiger Aero might have looked futuristic during its time, it is now nothing but nostalgia. Given how fast this AI design trend has developed, in a year or two, we will likely look back at these purple gradients and question our design decisions.

#### A deeper dive: perhaps the problem is not how we brand AI, but why we brand AI.

We are likely focusing too much on branding AI. AI deserves attention, but end users don't use AI for the sake of using AI. They use AI because they want to solve a problem or achieve a goal. In other words, integrating AI capabilities seamlessly into the overall offerings to help the users out is more important than throwing starbursts and gradients at them for AI awareness. Most of the time, users don't even need to know they are using AI.

Using Adobe Acrobat as an example, instead of introducing AI where it makes sense, Acrobat essentially discarded the established UI hierarchy and affordances to ensure users see its AI assistant button. Not only is this causes confusion for both new and existing users about the main function of the app, but it also emphasizes the AI's presence instead of how it can help.

But perhaps this is just a transition phase. One may argue that it is necessary for a company's marketing and UX teams to [find the balance](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ux-and-marketing/ "") to jump on the trend for the hype, for the stock price, and to get their users excited.

If you ever wondered "How else would we brand an AI product," ChatGPT and Claude are both great examples to show how AI doesn't need to scream AI to be useful to the users. Next year, their brands and UI will likely still look evergreen and remain relevant, while other brands suffer from a lack of uniqueness and user fatigue due to the overuse of design elements once considered 'trendy'.

We don't know what the future holds for us but if there is one 'trend' that doesn't fade and only evolves, it's usability. As cliché as it sounds, if there needs to be a starburst, let it be the one from the usability north star. ✨
